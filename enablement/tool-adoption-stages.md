# Tool Adoption Stages (v1)

When a new tool does not get used, people reach for two explanations: the tool is bad, or
the team is resistant. Most of the time it is neither. People move through stages when
they pick up something new, and a rollout stalls when it treats all of those stages as one.

This is a plain model of those stages. The point is to be able to say where someone is, so
you know what they actually need next instead of guessing.

## The five stages

### 1. Heard of it
They know the thing exists. That is all. They have seen the announcement or sat through the
demo, but they have not touched it.

- **Why people stay here:** nothing has made it their problem yet. It is one more message
  in a busy week.
- **What moves them up:** a reason that lands on something they already care about. Show the
  tool doing the annoying part of a job they actually do, not a generic tour.

### 2. Tried it once
They opened it, clicked around, maybe ran one real thing through it.

- **Why people stay here:** the first try was confusing, or it worked but going back means
  remembering where it was and how it went. The old way is still right there.
- **What moves them up:** remove the friction from the second use. A bookmark, a saved
  template, a one-line "here is exactly how to start." Make try number two cheaper than try
  number one.

### 3. Use it when reminded
They use it, but only when someone points them at it or a process tells them to.

- **Why people stay here:** it has not become the default in their head yet. Under time
  pressure they fall back to the familiar way.
- **What moves them up:** put it in the path of the work. Make it the obvious next step in a
  checklist or handoff, so using it is the easy choice rather than an extra one.

### 4. Reach for it on their own
They choose the tool without being told, because it is now the fastest way to get the job
done.

- **Why people stay here (and that is fine):** this is a healthy place to be. The risk is
  quiet drift, where a change or a bug pushes them back to the old way and nobody notices.
- **What moves them up:** keep it dependable and listen for friction. A reliable tool earns
  the next stage on its own.

### 5. Show someone else
They teach a colleague, suggest an improvement, or defend the tool in a meeting. They have
become an owner, not only a user.

- **Why this matters:** these people carry adoption further than any rollout plan. One of
  them is worth more than another announcement.
- **What to do:** notice them, give them room, and route real improvements through them.

## How to use this

- Adoption is not one number. At any moment your users are spread across these stages.
- A stalled rollout almost always has a pile-up at one stage. Find the pile-up, then apply
  the move for that stage instead of pushing harder on everyone at once.
- Different stages need different help. Telling a stage-1 person "use it more" does nothing,
  because they have no reason to yet.
