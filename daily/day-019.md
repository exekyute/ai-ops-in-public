# Day 019 - Analyze

**Date:** 2026-07-10
**Pillar:** Analyze

## Tidbit

The send-back rate has read like a real health number for two weeks, and building the thing that watches
it is what showed me its blind spot. A metric that can be gamed needs a second metric watching the gate,
or the first one quietly measures nothing. Defining the review miss rate today forced one hard admission:
it is a rate on a deliberately biased sample. The spot-audit aims at approvals because that is where a lax
review hides, so the number is a detector, not an average, and reading it as an average would fool me
worse than not having it at all. The general lesson is one Day 012 already taught, a metric inherits the
honesty of the process that feeds it, and the fix for a gameable metric is a second number pointed at the
gap the first one cannot see, not a slicker version of the first. The sharper edge this week: the better a
metric is at catching one specific failure, the worse it works as a general estimate, and forgetting that
is its own way of fooling yourself.

## Shipped

`analytics/metric-definitions.md` taken to **v3** with a third metric, **Review miss rate**: of the
reviews the spot-audit re-reviewed in a period, the share the audit overturned. It is the second signal
read next to the send-back rate, the number that separates a rubber stamp from real prep, because a
near-zero send-back rate reads the same whether builds arrive ready or the reviewer stopped looking, and a
high miss rate under it is the tell. The entry follows the same shape as the other two (what it is, why,
formula, what counts, data source, cadence, how to read it without fooling yourself, a worked synthetic
example, targets). It reads the audit fields the Day 018 review-log spec left room for, so this metric is
what defines what those fields must carry. The honest core: it is a rate on a small sample deliberately
weighted toward approvals, so it is a detector aimed where misses hide, not an unbiased estimate; the
false-approval subset (an approval the audit would send back) is pulled out as the number the send-back
rate cannot see; and a per-reviewer pattern matters more than the pooled rate. It stays distinct from
exception rate (runtime) and send-back rate (build prep); the scope line broadened and a v3 changelog
records the change; the two existing entries were left byte-identical.
