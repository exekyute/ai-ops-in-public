# Day 016 - Govern

**Date:** 2026-07-07
**Pillar:** Govern

## Tidbit

Every control I had built pointed the same direction, at the builder, and each one quietly assumed the
person doing the checking was honest. The worked example on Monday made that assumption visible: the
whole loop's integrity rested on the reviewer actually running the checks, and nothing in the loop
checked the reviewer. A control that checks one side and trusts the other relocates trust rather than
removing it. The fix is a control pointed the other way, a spot-audit that re-reviews a sample of decided
builds by someone who was not the original reviewer. What I had to accept while writing it is that you
cannot audit everyone, because then you have to trust the auditors and the problem climbs another rung.
So the audit is partial on purpose: it makes a lazy review detectable and raises its cost. It does not
make one impossible. That is the shape of governance I keep relearning: you rarely remove trust, you
price it and make its abuse visible.

## Shipped

`governance/review-spot-audit.md` (v1): the guardrail on the reviewer, the way the checklist is the
guardrail on the builder. It closes the loop's one open assumption, that the review itself is honest,
which Monday's worked example and the week 2 map both named. The mechanism is a spot-audit: a second
person, not the original reviewer and not the builder, re-reviews a small random sample of already-decided
builds against the same SOP and checklist, blind to the original marks, then compares. A different
decision is a miss, and the miss that matters most is an approval the audit would have sent back, because
that is the one the send-back rate cannot see. The sample is weighted toward approvals, where a lax review
hides, and the audit is read next to the send-back rate: a near-zero rate with clean audits is real prep,
a near-zero rate with misses is a rubber stamp. It is honest that it is partial by design: auditing
everyone would move the trust up a rung, so the audit makes a lazy review detectable rather than
impossible. It leaves two threads for later: a review miss rate metric in analytics, and a review log with
a reviewer field to sample from.
