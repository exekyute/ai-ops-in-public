# Day 026 - Analyze

**Date:** 2026-07-17
**Pillar:** Analyze

## Tidbit

Every metric in this file has a way of lying, and each one lies differently. Exception rate cannot see the
run that never fired. Send-back rate cannot see the approval that should have bounced. Review latency, it
turns out, cannot see the build that is still waiting, because a median over finished work counts only the
builds that finished. That is the worst shape a queue number can have: the more the queue rots, the better
the median looks, since the rotting builds never enter it. The pattern I keep finding is that a metric's
blind spot sits exactly where its data stops, and the fix is never a cleverer formula, it is a second
number that reads a different record. Here the median reads the log and the oldest-waiting age reads the
queue, which is why they have to be read together. The sharpest thing the drafting turned up was that
latency and the miss rate go blind at the same place: a pool of one posts the fastest median in the file,
because a builder reviewing their own build clears instantly, and no audit exists to catch it. Count the
people before you read either number.

## Shipped

`analytics/metric-definitions.md` taken to **v4** with a fourth metric, **Review latency**: the median wait
from handoff to first-review decision, the first entry here that measures a duration rather than a share.
Its companion is the oldest-waiting age (now minus the handoff time of the longest-waiting build still in
the queue), read beside the median every time, because the median is a lagging number about builds that are
done waiting while the oldest-waiting age reads the live queue. The entry's center is the survivorship
trap: undecided builds are excluded by construction, so a queue clearing its easy builds while the hard
ones rot posts a fast median. The worked example makes that bite, 7 first reviews with a 3-day median while
6 builds sit undecided at 8 to 14 days, and all 13 in flight would have a middle of 8. It names a real
prerequisite, the handoff time survives nowhere durable (the log has the decision date, the queue row has
the handoff time and is deleted when logged), so the log needs one new field. And it pairs latency with the
review miss rate, since latency can always be cut by reviewing worse, and notes both go blind at a pool too
small to be independent. The three shipped entries were left byte-identical.
