# Day 017 - Enable

**Date:** 2026-07-08
**Pillar:** Enable

## Tidbit

Governance builds the mechanism; enablement decides whether it survives contact with people. The
spot-audit I wrote yesterday is only as good as the conversation a miss triggers, and that conversation
has a perverse failure mode. Deliver a miss as a charge and the reviewer's rational move is to stop making
real calls, either rubber-stamp everything or send everything back, which destroys the exact signal the
audit was built to protect. So the guide's whole job is to keep a miss a calibration and not a verdict.
The deeper lesson is that a control which punishes the honesty it depends on gets that honesty withdrawn.
You can design a clean audit and still kill it in the first conversation, because the people layer is
where the mechanism lives or dies, not a step downstream of it.

## Shipped

`enablement/handling-a-review-miss.md` (v1): the people-side companion to the spot-audit, how to run the
auditor-reviewer conversation after a miss so it sharpens the next review instead of souring it. The core
move is to name the kind of miss together before anyone talks about a fix: a judgment call, where the
standard left room and two careful reviewers read a build differently, which calibrates where the line
sits; or a real gap, where a check got read instead of run (the eyeballed idempotency item from the worked
example), which fixes a habit. The steps keep it on the build, not the reviewer: open from the build,
settle the right call against the standard (the audit has to be able to lose), name the kind, agree the
smallest change, keep the record light and shared. It names the failure modes that turn a reviewer
defensive: a miss delivered as a verdict, an auditor treated as infallible, and misses counted as a
scoreboard. It ends honest: a guide cannot manufacture good faith, and the first miss conversation is the
real test of whether the audit survives.
